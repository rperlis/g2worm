About G2Worm: Human GWAS to C Elegans

Repurposing existing FDA-approved medications for novel indications presents substantial advantages: The safety and dose range has already been established, so the path to clinical practice is far shorter. However, one challenge is extrapolating from in vitro models to humans: does the drug get into the brain? To complement traditional PK approaches (i.e., dose the rat, then chop its head off), chemical properties such as molecular weight may be useful. This calculator uses an alternate approach, based on the assumption that drugs which enter brain are likely to have adverse effect profiles similar to other brain-penetrant drugs. This is a heuristic, not a perfect solution. However, it may be useful in prioritizing among a group of medications being considered for further study in vivo. The calculator implements the similarity estimates described in Perlis, in review. For details, see that manuscript. Original pairwise similarities drawn from Tatonetti, Sci Trans Med 2012.

Notes
General notes
The calculator implements the similarity estimates described in Perlis, in review. These are calculated as the mean of similarity z-scores with the comparison drugs selected. For details, see that manuscript. Original pairwise similarities drawn from Tatonetti, Sci Trans Med 2012. Select either a predefined comparison panel (antidepressant, antipsychotic, or the combination), or enter a comma-separated list in the custom box (don't forget to select custom from the pulldown, too). The 'random' pulldown option will select 3 random drugs for comparison; it illustrates that the importance of selecting an appropriate comparison panel.
Pairwise data notes
The original pairwise similarity measures and z-scores are accessible via Tatonetti et al, Sci Trans Med 2012; this data set includes ~631 drugs. Side effects reflect both package insert and postmarketing data.
Chemistry notes
Additional chemical property data (molecular weight and log-P) are drawn from Pubchem, last updated 10/20/2012.
